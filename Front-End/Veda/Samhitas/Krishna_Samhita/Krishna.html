Understanding Krishna Samhitas

The Krishna Samhitas are a collection of sacred texts that form an integral part of ancient Vedic literature. These scriptures contain the divine knowledge, philosophical insights, and spiritual teachings attributed to Lord Krishna, who is revered as the eighth avatar of Lord Vishnu in Hindu tradition.

These texts serve as a spiritual guide, offering profound wisdom on dharma (righteousness), karma (action), bhakti (devotion), and moksha (liberation). They provide a comprehensive framework for understanding the nature of existence, the relationship between the individual soul and the supreme consciousness, and the path to spiritual enlightenment.

The most renowned of these texts is the Bhagavad Gita, often referred to as the "Song of God," which forms a section of the epic Mahabharata. It captures the dialogue between Lord Krishna and the warrior Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, addressing the moral and philosophical dilemmas of human existence.

Key Texts in Krishna Samhitas

Bhagavad Gita

The most celebrated dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna, consisting of 700 verses divided into 18 chapters. It addresses questions about duty, righteousness, and spiritual liberation.

Learn more

Srimad Bhagavatam

Also known as Bhagavata Purana, this text contains the complete biography of Krishna and his teachings. It consists of 12 cantos with approximately 18,000 verses.

Learn more

Gita Govinda

Composed by Jayadeva in the 12th century, this lyrical poem describes the relationship between Krishna and Radha, symbolizing the divine love between God and the human soul.

Learn more

Hari Vamsa

Considered an appendix to the Mahabharata, this text focuses on the genealogy and early life of Krishna, including his childhood adventures and heroic deeds.

Learn more

Vishnu Purana

One of the eighteen Mahapuranas, this text contains extensive accounts of Krishna's life and teachings, emphasizing his role as the preserver of cosmic order.

Learn more

Brahma Samhita

A collection of prayers offered by Lord Brahma to Govinda (Krishna), revealing the supreme position of Krishna as the original personality of Godhead.

Historical Timeline

1

3000 BCE - The Mahabharata Era

The events described in the Mahabharata, including the dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna that forms the Bhagavad Gita, are traditionally believed to have occurred during this period.

2

500 BCE - 500 CE - Compilation Period

During this extensive period, many of the Krishna-related texts were compiled, edited, and formalized into the versions we recognize today. The oral traditions were systematically documented.

3

8th-9th Century CE - Adi Shankaracharya

The renowned philosopher Adi Shankaracharya wrote commentaries on the Bhagavad Gita, bringing philosophical depth and renewed interest to Krishna's teachings.

4

12th Century CE - Bhakti Movement

The Bhakti movement gained momentum, with poets like Jayadeva composing the Gita Govinda, celebrating Krishna's divine love. This period saw increased devotional focus on Krishna.

5

15th-16th Century CE - Chaitanya Mahaprabhu

Chaitanya Mahaprabhu led a revival of Krishna consciousness, emphasizing devotional service and the chanting of Krishna's names. His movement significantly influenced the understanding and practice of Krishna's teachings.

6

20th Century CE - Global Spread

The teachings of Krishna gained international recognition through the efforts of scholars and spiritual leaders like A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, who translated and commented on many Krishna Samhitas.
