Ancient Wisdom of the Rigveda

Explore the oldest of the Vedas, a collection of hymns and mantras that form the foundation of ancient Indian knowledge and spirituality.

Introduction to Rigveda Samhita

The Rigveda is an ancient Indian collection of Vedic Sanskrit hymns. It is one of the four sacred canonical texts (śruti) of Hinduism known as the Vedas. The text is a collection of 1,028 hymns and 10,600 verses, organized into ten books (mandalas).

The Rigveda was composed between approximately 1700–1100 BCE, making it one of the oldest religious texts still in use. The hymns are dedicated to Rigvedic deities and discuss cosmology, praise deities, and contain philosophical speculations.

The Rigveda Samhita is available in several recensions (shakhas), of which the Shakala and the Ashvalayana are the most prominent. Each recension has its own unique features and interpretations of the hymns.

Structure of the Rigveda

The Rigveda is organized into ten mandalas (books), which contain hymns called suktas. Each sukta consists of individual stanzas called riks, which give the Rigveda its name. The organization of the Rigveda is as follows:

  • Mandalas 2-7: Each mandala contains hymns attributed to a single rishi (sage) family
  • Mandala 1: Collection of hymns from various authors
  • Mandala 8: Contains the Valakhilya hymns
  • Mandala 9: Dedicated to Soma Pavamana
  • Mandala 10: Contains philosophical hymns and speculations

Important Hymns and Concepts

Some of the most significant hymns in the Rigveda include:

Purusha Sukta

This hymn describes the sacrifice of the cosmic being Purusha, from whose body the world and the four varnas (social classes) were created.

Nasadiya Sukta

Also known as the "Hymn of Creation," it questions the origin of the universe and introduces philosophical concepts about existence.

Gayatri Mantra

One of the most sacred mantras in Hinduism, dedicated to Savitr, the sun deity, asking for illumination of the intellect.

Agni Sukta

The very first hymn of the Rigveda, dedicated to Agni, the fire deity who acts as a messenger between humans and gods.

Shakala Samhita

40% Complete

The most widely available recension of the Rigveda, containing all 10 mandalas with 1,028 hymns.

Ashvalayana Samhita

20% Complete

A less common recension with unique interpretations and arrangement of the hymns.
